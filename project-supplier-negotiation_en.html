Background & Challenges

Situation: Quality Issues from a New Supplier

We began sourcing from a new overseas parts supplier, but immediately faced a situation where the defect rate of delivered goods reached a high level. Our quality department's correction requests did not receive a sufficient response, threatening to cause disruptions to our production schedule.

Difficulty: Communication & "Quality" Perception Gap

The biggest problem was a difference in the perception of quality. The supplier had a culture of "some defects are acceptable; we'll just replace them if they're a problem," and they did not fully understand the high-quality level required by Japanese manufacturers. Email exchanges did not progress, and communication became challenging. The greatest challenge was bridging this cultural background difference to establish a common goal.

Approach to Resolution

Aligning Policy with the End Customer

First, we contacted the final product manufacturer to reaffirm the quality policy required for the product. This verified our quality standards were aligned with their requirements and strengthened our position for negotiation.

Three-Party Talks with Direct Customer

To break the stalemate, we requested cooperation from our direct customer. We arranged a meeting abroad to discuss the quality issues among all three parties: our customer, our company, and the supplier. This created a situation the supplier could not disregard.

On-Site Factory Visit for Direct Dialogue

After the meeting, we visited the supplier's factory directly. We approached with a "partner" mindset, not an adversarial one, and reviewed the manufacturing process together. Seeing the site firsthand clarified the issues, and we were able to confirm their willingness to "make a good product."

Rebuilding the Partnership

Through dialogue, we jointly established specific quality standards. We agreed on an inspection system to filter out non-conforming items before shipment. Furthermore, we established regular communication channels to discuss process improvements, ensuring an open line of communication.

Takeaways & Replicability

Find Common Goals and Resolve Issues with Dialogue

Regardless of cultural or business differences, the goal to "make a good final product" was universal. Initially, we judged each other based on emails and fell into an adversarial situation. By meeting in person and looking at the process from the same perspective, we were able to build a cooperative relationship as "partners" with a common goal. I learned that even in difficult negotiations, a solution can be found by respecting the other's culture and striving to find common ground.

Skills Used

Replication Points

  • Involve Upstream Customers: Align on quality policy across the entire supply chain to validate your position.

  • From Conflict to Collaboration: Visit the site as a "partner," not an "adversary," to show a commitment to solving problems together.

  • Be On-Site (Genba): Go beyond emails and meetings. Be mindful to see the actual site, check the actual parts, and understand the actual situation.
