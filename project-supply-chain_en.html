Transportation Route Optimization via Triangular Trade

Background & Challenges

Situation: An Ingrained Inefficient Route

For years, it was standard practice to ship components procured from a Thai supplier to our home country hub before forwarding them to our US manufacturing site. This inefficient double-shipping route had become a long-standing custom, inflating not only transportation costs but also unnecessarily extending lead times.

Difficulty: Complex Trilateral Coordination

Implementing triangular trade was more than just changing freight forwarders. The legal, tax, and customs requirements of the involved countries were intricately intertwined. Determining who was responsible for which documents and how to structure the contracts proved difficult. It took a significant amount of time to persuade the relevant departments and secure their cooperation.

Our Approach to Resolution

Data Analysis: Visualizing and Quantifying the Problem

We began by questioning the "way it's always been done." By thoroughly analyzing past shipping data, we broke down the costs and lead times. We presented objective evidence that visualized the inefficiency of the double-shipping route (Thailand to a transit hub, then to the US), allowing all stakeholders to grasp the magnitude of the problem.

Solution Seeking & Negotiation: Finding the Optimal Path

We identified "triangular trade" – shipping directly from Thailand to the US – as the key solution. We contacted multiple international forwarders to inquire about the feasibility and cost of a direct route. After a multi-faceted comparison of quotes and service levels, we selected the best partner in terms of both cost and quality.

Cross-Departmental Collaboration: Breaking Down Invisible Barriers

Realizing triangular trade required overcoming not just logistical hurdles, but legal and tax barriers as well. We formed a team that worked closely with legal and customs representatives in the involved countries. One by one, we cleared the requirements of each country's regulations, tariffs, and necessary paperwork, building a compliant foundation for the new route.

Validation & Rollout: From Hypothesis to Certainty

As a final step, we conducted a pilot shipment with a small amount of cargo. This demonstrated that the plan on paper would function smoothly in actual operations. Following this success, we moved to full-scale implementation, establishing stable operations and contributing to company-wide cost savings and delivery time improvements.

Takeaways & Replicability

Data is a Universal Language

From this experience, I learned the importance of using data to uncover "hidden inefficiencies" in existing workflows. I also realized that when involving many stakeholders across countries and departments, objective data serves as a common language and a powerful tool for facilitating smooth consensus-building.

Skills Used

Replication Points

  • Question the Status Quo: Quantitatively evaluate "long-standing customs" with data to visualize inefficiencies.

  • Map Your Stakeholders: Identify all parties necessary for a solution and involve them early on.

  • Start with a Small Trial: Conduct a pilot shipment before full-scale implementation to verify feasibility while minimizing risk.
