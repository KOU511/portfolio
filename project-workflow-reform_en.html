Background & Challenges

Situation: Building a Process with No Precedent

With the launch of a new business, it was necessary to build an unprecedented cross-departmental workflow. However, each department was trying to operate based on its own norms, and the absence of a unified process raised concerns about future rework and interdepartmental conflicts.

Difficulty: Consensus Building Without a Right Answer

The greatest difficulty was consolidating the opinions of stakeholders with diverse expertise to guide them toward a single, optimal process, especially when no one yet knew the right answer. Conflicting beliefs that "my department's way is the most efficient" often led to deadlocked discussions.

Our Approach to Resolution

Visualize Key Issues: Creating a "Map of Thoughts"

Through interviews with team members from each department, we mapped out their assumed workflows and expectations of other departments on an online whiteboard. This "map of thoughts" created a foundation for everyone to share and understand the invisible points of contention, such as misaligned perceptions and communication gaps between departments.

Facilitating Consensus: Dialogue Based on the Map

We held workshops with all relevant departments, projecting the completed map onto a screen. By facilitating dialogue based on the map—asking questions like, "So, this is Department A's ideal output, correct?" and "And for that to happen, you need this information from Department B, is that right?"—we promoted constructive, fact-based discussions rather than emotional conflicts.

Co-creating the Ideal Flow: Drawing the Future Map

Using the online whiteboard again, this time we designed the "ideal workflow" together. As we incorporated ideas and concerns from each department in real-time, we collectively drew a new process that was efficient and allowed for a smooth flow of information. This co-creation process fostered a sense of ownership over the new workflow.

Standardization and Implementation: Turning the Map into "Rules"

Based on the ideal flow, we created an official operational procedure manual that was easy for anyone to understand. By obtaining approval from the heads of all relevant departments, we established this as the "standard process" for the new business. This ensured that the improvements were not a one-time fix but were embedded as formal rules for the organization.

Value Delivered

Speedy Business Launch

The standardized flow created a foundation for a smooth and rapid new business launch.

Smoother Collaboration

Clear roles and responsibilities reduced communication loss and conflict between departments.

High-Quality Execution

A system was established to ensure consistent quality regardless of who was in charge, eliminating dependency on individuals.

Ensured Scalability

We established a highly extensible process that can flexibly adapt to business expansion and staffing increases.

Takeaways & Replicability

A "Compass" in Uncertainty

I learned that in a highly uncertain new business venture, process visualization serves as a compass. By mapping out the tacit assumptions and goals in each stakeholder's mind, we create a foundation for constructive discussion. I am convinced that this is a powerful tool for elevating scattered individual ideas into a unified team goal.

Skills Used

Replication Points

  • Visualize First: Map out stakeholders' thoughts and existing processes onto an objective "map."

  • Dialogue Based on the Map: Avoid emotional conflicts and promote constructive, fact-based discussions.

  • Create Together: Instead of imposing a top-down solution, involve all stakeholders in a workshop format to "co-create."
